# 100 Network Effect Viral Growth Hacks

**Based on Andrew Chen's philosophy** — from *The Cold Start Problem*, his years running Rider Growth at Uber, and his a16z essays on viral loops, the Law of Shitty Clickthroughs, and network effects.

These are organized around Chen's five stages of network effects: **Cold Start → Tipping Point → Escape Velocity → Hitting the Ceiling → The Moat.** Use them as brainstorming fuel — steal the pattern, not the tactic.

---

## Part 1: Solve the Cold Start Problem with Atomic Networks (1–10)

1. **Shrink your network to the smallest unit that works.** Chen's core insight: don't launch to "everyone." Slack's atomic network was a single team of 3 people. Find your version of "3 people on one team" and obsess over making that tiny unit successful.

2. **Launch to one campus, one city, one company.** Facebook started at Harvard. Uber started in San Francisco. Tinder started at USC. Density beats reach — 500 users in one zip code is a network; 5,000 users spread across a country is a ghost town.

3. **Do things that don't scale to fake the network.** Reddit's founders posted under dozens of fake usernames to make the site feel alive. Paypal built a bot that bought items on eBay and paid with PayPal. Seed the supply yourself before real supply exists.

4. **Use the "flintstone" approach.** Behind the scenes, humans manually do what the product promises to automate — DoorDash founders delivered food themselves. It buys you time until network density catches up.

5. **Throw a party, not a product launch.** Tinder's team drove to USC sorority houses, signed up the sororities, then went to the frat houses and showed them who was already on. Recruit one side of the network in person, then sell the other side on its existence.

6. **Pick a "hard side magnet" as your first feature.** Build the one feature the most valuable users desperately need, even if it's niche. Twitch began as Justin.tv focusing on gamers streaming — the hard-side creators came for the tool built precisely for them.

7. **Constrain the product to force density.** Invite-only launches (Gmail, Clubhouse, Robinhood's waitlist) aren't just hype — they concentrate early users into tight, connected clusters and make each invite feel like social currency.

8. **Buy or rent your first network.** Airbnb famously piggybacked Craigslist listings. Zoom spread inside companies via one free user inviting a meeting guest. Find where your atomic network already congregates and siphon it.

9. **Give the first users superpowers.** Early Yelp Elite got parties and badges; early Uber drivers got guaranteed hourly pay regardless of trips. Overpay the pioneers — they're not customers, they're infrastructure.

10. **Define your network's "minimum viable density" metric.** Uber's was pickup ETA under a threshold in a neighborhood. Slack's was 2,000 messages sent by a team. Know the exact number at which your atomic network becomes self-sustaining, and drive only that number.

## Part 2: Win the Hard Side of the Network (11–20)

11. **Identify who your "hard side" is and serve them 10x.** Chen: every network has a hard side (drivers, creators, sellers, hosts) that does more work and is harder to acquire. Uber spent the majority of growth dollars on drivers, not riders. Figure out which side is scarce and pour resources there.

12. **Court the top 1% of creators like enterprise sales.** YouTube, TikTok, and Substack all cut direct deals with star creators. Treat your top hard-side users as named accounts with account managers, not as rows in a database.

13. **Build "side hustle" economics for the hard side.** Uber's pitch to drivers wasn't a career — it was flexible extra income. Airbnb's was "your spare room pays your mortgage." Frame hard-side participation as found money with low commitment.

14. **Subsidize the hard side until liquidity arrives.** Uber paid drivers guaranteed rates before demand existed. Marketplaces should run intentionally unprofitable early economics on the scarce side — it's customer acquisition cost, not loss.

15. **Give the hard side status, not just money.** Leaderboards, verified badges, "Superhost" tiers, creator funds with public rankings. Status compounds and costs you nothing marginal.

16. **Reduce hard-side friction to near zero.** Every form field you remove from seller onboarding is a growth hack. Shopify, Stripe, and Substack won by making the hard thing (starting a store, accepting payments, publishing) take minutes.

17. **Let the hard side bring their own audience.** Substack's genius: writers arrive with their email lists. Patreon creators bring their fans. Design onboarding so hard-side users import their existing network on day one.

18. **Build tools the hard side uses even off-network.** Give creators analytics, scheduling, editing tools they'd use anyway. OpenTable gave restaurants a booking system before diners showed up — the tool was the wedge.

19. **Protect hard-side earnings when you change anything.** Chen notes networks hit revolts (Uber driver protests, YouTube "adpocalypse"). Grandfather economics, communicate early — churn on the hard side is 10x more expensive than the easy side.

20. **Recruit the hard side from rival networks' unhappy moments.** When a competitor changes its fee structure or algorithm, run targeted switching campaigns within days. Lyft grew fastest during every Uber PR crisis.

## Part 3: Come for the Tool, Stay for the Network (21–30)

21. **Ship a single-player tool that's valuable with zero other users.** Instagram was a filter app before it was a network. Dropbox was personal backup before shared folders. Nobody joins an empty network — everyone will use a great tool.

22. **Make the tool's output inherently shareable.** Instagram filters made photos worth posting elsewhere. Loom videos, Calendly links, Figma files — the artifact travels, carrying your brand and a signup prompt with it.

23. **Turn saved content into a public profile.** Pinterest turned private bookmarking into shareable boards. Letterboxd turned movie logging into a social identity. The private utility quietly builds the public network asset.

24. **Add collaboration as the upgrade path.** Notion, Figma, and Canva convert single players into networks by making "invite a teammate" the natural next step in the workflow — not a marketing prompt, but a functional necessity.

25. **Embed your product where work already happens.** Grammarly lives inside every text field; Honey inside checkout. Being an ambient layer over existing behavior means zero habit change needed.

26. **Watermark the free tier.** "Sent from my iPhone." "Made with Wix." Hotmail's "Get your free email at Hotmail" footer created the canonical viral loop. Your free users' output is your billboard — make removal a paid feature.

27. **Create files and links that require your app to open.** Figma links, Google Docs, Splitwise balances — when the artifact only fully works inside your product, every share is a forced signup event.

28. **Give away the tool that generates your network's inventory.** YouTube gave free unlimited video hosting when bandwidth was expensive. The tool subsidy fills the network with content that then attracts consumers.

29. **Let users get value before signing up.** Chen calls signup walls a top killer of viral loops. Let the invited user see the shared doc, the ride ETA, the listing — then gate the second action, not the first.

30. **Design a "single-use to habit" ladder.** Calendly: receive one booking link → book → notice how easy it was → make your own link. Map how a passive recipient becomes an active node, and grease each rung.

## Part 4: Engineer the Viral Loop (31–40)

31. **Compute your viral factor and attack each term separately.** Chen's classic model: new users = invites sent per user × click-through rate × conversion rate. A 10% improvement in each term compounds — treat the loop as a funnel with its own optimization team.

32. **Shorten cycle time above all else.** Chen's math shows loop speed beats loop branching factor: a loop that completes in 1 day beats a bigger loop that takes a week. Move the invite moment earlier; make invites deliver instantly (SMS beat email for Uber referrals).

33. **Put the invite inside the core use, not beside it.** Splitwise requires adding the friend who owes you money. Venmo requires a counterparty. WhatsApp groups require inviting members. The best invite is one the user sends for their own selfish reasons.

34. **Pre-fill everything in the invite flow.** Contact import, suggested recipients, pre-written messages, one-tap send. Every field the inviter must fill costs you exponential downstream users.

35. **Make the invitation itself the product demo.** A Loom invite is a video. A Canva invite is a design. A Partiful invite is the party page. The recipient experiences the value before they've signed up.

36. **Trigger invites at the peak-emotion moment.** Ask for shares right after the win: trip completed, design shipped, workout logged, money received. Never during onboarding, when the user has received zero value.

37. **Use "claim your money/gift" mechanics.** Venmo and PayPal send "you've been paid — sign up to claim it." An invite that withholds something the recipient already owns converts at rates marketing emails never touch.

38. **Build two-sided invite incentives.** Uber's "give $20, get $20" worked because the inviter never felt like they were spamming — they were giving a gift. One-sided rewards feel like exploitation; two-sided feels like generosity.

39. **Let groups form before individuals commit.** Group chats, party invites, fantasy leagues, group orders: one motivated organizer conscripts 5–10 semi-willing users. Design for the organizer persona — they're your true viral agent.

40. **Instrument and A/B test the loop weekly.** Uber's growth team tested referral copy, amounts, placement, and timing continuously. The viral loop decays as novelty fades (Law of Shitty Clickthroughs applies to invites too) — refresh it like ad creative.

## Part 5: Referral & Incentive Programs, Uber-Style (41–50)

41. **Denominate rewards in your own product, not cash.** Uber gave ride credits, not dollars — costs you margin, not face value, and forces another usage session that deepens habit.

42. **Vary referral bonuses by market maturity.** Uber paid huge driver referral bonuses in new cities and tapered them as density arrived. Treat incentive spend as a dial per atomic network, not one global number.

43. **Target referral offers at your power users.** The top decile of users generates the majority of successful referrals. Give them bigger rewards, custom codes, and vanity handles ("uber.com/yourname") instead of blasting everyone equally.

44. **Pay for the completed action, not the signup.** Reward when the invitee finishes their first ride/order/trade — this aligns the whole loop toward activation, not empty account creation, and kills fraud economics.

45. **Run "boost weeks" with expiring referral multipliers.** Temporary 2x referral bonuses create urgency spikes and let you A/B the price elasticity of your viral loop without permanently raising costs.

46. **Give creators/drivers recruitment quotas with escalators.** "Refer 3 drivers, get $500; refer 10, get $2,000 and Gold status." The hard side recruiting the hard side is the cheapest supply acquisition channel that exists.

47. **Turn the referral code into merch and IRL surface area.** Uber drivers handed out cards with their code; influencers put codes in bios. Give users physical and digital artifacts that carry their code — QR stickers, link-in-bio pages, business cards.

48. **Use milestone-gated unlocks instead of pure cash.** Dropbox's extra storage per referral is the canonical example: the reward increases product capacity, which increases usage, which increases exposure to sharing moments.

49. **Build fraud detection before scaling incentives.** Chen's Uber team fought massive referral fraud (burner phones, GPS spoofing). Every incentive program is an adversarial game — cap rewards, verify devices, delay payouts, or your viral metrics are fiction.

50. **Sunset incentives as network effects take over.** The point of paid referrals is to bootstrap organic pull, not replace it. Track the ratio of incentivized vs. organic joins per market and cut spend the moment organic dominates — Uber's biggest markets eventually needed no rider promos at all.

## Part 6: Tipping Point — Conquer Market by Market (51–60)

51. **Replicate your playbook city-by-city, not nationally.** Uber wrote a literal launch playbook per city: hire local GM, seed drivers with guarantees, run a launch event, flood promo codes. Codify your atomic-network recipe and stamp it repeatedly.

52. **Pick each next market where you already have gravitational pull.** Expand to adjacent networks: the next campus over, the industry that borders your current one, the city your users travel to most. Cold-starting near a warm network is half-price.

53. **Manufacture a lightning-strike launch event.** Superhuman's waitlist mystique, product-of-the-year awards, a stunt like Uber's on-demand ice cream trucks or kittens. A concentrated burst of attention tips a market that months of drip marketing won't.

54. **Use waitlists to convert scarcity into density.** Robinhood's referral-ranked waitlist got a million signups pre-launch. The waitlist sorts your most eager atomic network to the front and makes them recruit to move up the line.

55. **Subsidize whichever side is the bottleneck this week.** Chen describes Uber's constant rebalancing: driver incentives when ETAs rose, rider promos when cars idled. Build a dashboard that tells you which side of the market to pour money on, per market, in real time.

56. **Land big anchor tenants to tip B2B networks.** One flagship employer on your job board, one famous restaurant on your delivery app, one anchor university on your platform — anchors let you market their presence to fill in the long tail.

57. **Go narrow on use case to go dense on network.** Chen: "niche networks tip faster." Discord focused on gamers for years before broadening. Own 80% of a small pond before touching the ocean.

58. **Convert offline communities wholesale.** Onboard the whole run club, the whole church group, the whole trading Discord at once — sell to community leaders and give them admin tools, migration support, and revenue share.

59. **Time launches to external demand shocks.** Tax season for fintech, September for campus apps, conference season for B2B. A market at peak need tips at a fraction of the acquisition cost.

60. **Kill vanity geography.** Chen's Uber lesson in reverse: markets that never reach minimum density bleed subsidies forever. Exit or pause weak markets and recycle the spend into doubling density where you're near the tipping point.

## Part 7: Magic Moments & Activation (61–70)

61. **Define your magic moment and get users there in minutes.** Chen frames every great network product by its magic moment: car arrives in 3 minutes, your tweet gets replies, the empty room fills. Name yours, measure time-to-magic, and cut everything that stands before it.

62. **Onboard by building the user's network first.** Twitter's "follow 20 accounts" onboarding wasn't a nicety — it was the activation. For any network product, onboarding equals connecting, not touring features.

63. **Use contact-graph matching aggressively but respectfully.** "3 of your friends are already here" is the highest-converting notification in social products. Surface existing connections at signup, first session, and re-engagement.

64. **Never show an empty network — show the neighborhood.** Zero-follower feeds kill retention. Fill day-one experience with algorithmic content, nearby activity, or popular items so the network feels alive before the user's own graph exists.

65. **Send the "you got attention" notification within the first day.** New Facebook users had to reach 7 friends in 10 days; new creators need one like, comment, or view fast. Engineer early social feedback — even if you have to route community members to welcome newcomers.

66. **Create rituals that synchronize the network.** BeReal's simultaneous daily prompt, Wordle's one-per-day, Strava's weekly challenges. Synchronized behavior creates concentrated activity spikes that make the network feel dense even when it's small.

67. **Recast notifications as network activity, not app marketing.** "Sarah commented on your photo" outperforms "Check out what's new!" by an order of magnitude. Every push should carry another human's action.

68. **Build streaks and reciprocity mechanics.** Snapchat streaks made two-person retention contracts. Design features where my engagement obligates your engagement — the network itself becomes the retention system.

69. **Identify your "7 friends in 10 days" number.** Find the leading indicator that predicts long-term retention (friends added, messages sent, follows made, first sale) and make your entire growth org accountable to it — not to signups.

70. **Re-onboard dormant users through their network.** Win-back emails fail; "your friend just joined" and "your post got a new comment" succeed. Wake the dormant node by activating the edges connected to it.

## Part 8: Escape Velocity — Compounding Engagement Loops (71–80)

71. **Layer loops: acquisition, engagement, and economics.** Chen: escape velocity isn't one viral loop, it's three reinforcing effects — more users → more value (engagement effect), more users → easier acquisition (viral effect), more users → better unit economics (economic effect). Audit which of the three you're missing and build it deliberately.

72. **Turn user-generated content into SEO acquisition.** Every public profile, review, question, playlist, and listing is a landing page. Tripadvisor, Quora, Yelp, and Glassdoor built billion-user funnels from content their networks produced for free.

73. **Recycle in-network content to out-of-network channels.** TikTok watermarks its videos, which flood Instagram and YouTube as ads for TikTok. Make exporting easy and branded — your users' cross-posting is your distribution.

74. **Build embeds and widgets that live on other people's sites.** YouTube's embed player won the video wars. Spotify embeds, Twitter cards, review widgets — every embed is a beachhead on someone else's traffic.

75. **Move users up the engagement ladder deliberately.** Lurker → liker → commenter → creator. Design explicit graduation prompts at each rung (easy reaction tools, reply templates, remix buttons) because each level up multiplies the content that retains ten lurkers below.

76. **Use data network effects to personalize.** Every ride improves ETAs; every listen improves recommendations; every swipe improves matching. Instrument your product so usage automatically improves the experience — then tell users "your feed learns" so they invest more.

77. **Cross-side notifications to spin the marketplace flywheel.** Tell hosts when searches in their area spike; tell buyers when sellers they follow list something. Each side's activity should generate the other side's triggers.

78. **Open an API and platform layer.** Slack's app directory, Shopify's app store, Stripe's ecosystem: third-party developers add features you didn't build and switching costs you didn't pay for. Platforms are engagement loops staffed by other companies.

79. **Introduce a currency, karma, or reputation system.** Reddit karma, eBay ratings, Airbnb reviews, Duolingo gems. Accumulated non-transferable value is both an engagement loop and an exit barrier — people don't abandon what they've earned.

80. **Compound frequency: attach your product to a daily behavior.** Chen's hierarchy: daily-use products build networks fastest. If your core use is episodic (travel, job hunting), add a daily companion (price alerts, salary insights, community feed) to keep edges warm between transactions.

## Part 9: Fight the Law of Shitty Clickthroughs (81–90)

81. **Assume every channel decays — build a channel portfolio.** Chen's Law of Shitty Clickthroughs: all marketing channels degrade as novelty fades and competition bids up costs. Whatever's working now is temporary; always be incubating the next two channels.

82. **Be first to the new platform's growth loophole.** Zynga on early Facebook feeds, Airbnb on Craigslist, brands on early TikTok: each new platform briefly over-distributes content. Ride the arbitrage hard, but with an exit plan for when the platform closes the tap.

83. **Rebuild the banner ad as native content.** When a format's CTR dies, the answer is deeper integration: influencer posts instead of display ads, community presence instead of interruption. Move down the stack from advertising to participation.

84. **Refresh creative on a decay schedule.** Track each channel's CTR half-life and pre-produce creative refreshes to land before the decay bites. Uber's growth team rotated referral messaging like a media company.

85. **Counter-position against incumbents' notification fatigue.** Users tune out incumbents' spammy pushes. Send dramatically fewer, dramatically better notifications and say so publicly — inbox restraint is a differentiator when rivals have burned trust.

86. **Own the channel: build an email/SMS list from day one.** Rented audiences (algorithmic feeds, app store rankings) decay or get repriced. Substack writers survived platform shifts because email is a direct edge to the user that no algorithm can throttle.

87. **Turn your best-performing paid channel into a referral surface.** When paid CAC rises, redirect that budget into referral bonuses — your users' invite conversion rates don't decay the way ad CTRs do, because trust doesn't banner-blind.

88. **Exploit "boring" channels competitors abandoned.** Direct mail for fintech, podcasts for B2B, SMS for local services, flyers at trade shows. Channels everyone left have reset to high attention and low cost.

89. **Ship growth features, not growth campaigns.** Campaigns decay; product loops compound. Every quarter, convert your best campaign insight into a permanent product mechanic (a share flow, a public artifact, an invite trigger).

90. **Measure blended CAC by cohort and market, not averages.** Chen's Uber dashboards cut spend by city and segment. Averages hide the fact that channel decay hits your newest markets first — instrument granularly enough to see the rot early.

## Part 10: Hit the Ceiling, Build the Moat (91–100)

91. **Watch for ceiling signals before growth stalls.** Chen lists them: rising CAC, spam-filled feeds, trolls, market saturation, degraded quality. Assign a team to each degradation vector before the curve bends, not after.

92. **Fight context collapse by adding sub-networks.** When the network gets too big, intimacy dies. Facebook added Groups; Discord has servers; Reddit has subreddits. Let the mega-network fragment into thousands of fresh atomic networks inside your walls.

93. **Re-run the cold start playbook inside your own product.** Every new feature, geography, and vertical is its own cold start problem. Instagram launched Stories to its existing graph; Uber launched Eats on its driver network. Your existing network is the launchpad — use it deliberately.

94. **Prune the network to protect quality.** Ban spammers aggressively, cull fake listings, tighten creator standards. Chen: overcrowding and low quality are how networks die from the inside. Subtraction is a growth strategy at scale.

95. **Raise switching costs with accumulated network assets.** Stored reviews, follower graphs, message history, integrations, saved preferences. Make the user's investment portable *in* but not painlessly portable *out* — while staying on the right side of user trust.

96. **Bundle new products into the existing network.** Microsoft bundled Teams into Office and reversed Slack's momentum. When you own the network, distribution of adjacent products is nearly free — attach, don't launch.

97. **Compete for the hard side in wars of attrition.** Chen: network battles are won by whoever holds the scarce side. When rivals attack, concentrate subsidies and exclusive deals on your top creators/drivers/sellers — losing the top 1% of the hard side collapses the whole market.

98. **Cherry-pick a rival network's densest cluster.** Don't attack an incumbent broadly — peel off one high-value niche with a 10x better dedicated experience, the way Discord peeled gamers off Skype, then expand from that beachhead.

99. **Never confuse network effects with virality.** Chen is emphatic: virality is a growth rate; network effects are a retention and defensibility property. Audit them separately — a viral product with no network effect fills a leaky bucket faster (Chen's growth accounting: growth = acquisition + resurrection − churn).

100. **Rebuild the flywheel forever.** Chen ends *The Cold Start Problem* with this: even the biggest networks (MySpace, Skype, Craigslist) died from complacency. Institutionalize the cold-start muscle — a standing team whose job is to launch new atomic networks every year, inside or outside the core product.
