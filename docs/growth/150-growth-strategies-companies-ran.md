# 150 Growth Strategies, Tips & Tricks That Real Companies Ran

A company-first companion to the Andrew Chen list. Every entry here is a tactic a real company actually executed — steal the mechanic, not the surface. Numbers are the widely reported figures; treat them as directional.

---

## Part 1: Referral Programs That Actually Ran (1–10)

1. **Dropbox's two-sided storage bounty.** Give 500 megabytes, get 500 megabytes, up to 16 gigabytes free. Referrals drove 35 percent of daily signups and roughly 3900 percent growth in 15 months. The reward was product capacity, which cost pennies and deepened usage.

2. **PayPal literally paid you cash.** 20 dollars to sign up, 20 dollars per referral, later stepped down to ten and then five as density arrived. It bought 7 to 10 percent daily growth when nothing else could bootstrap a payments network.

3. **Harry's milestone ladder.** The pre-launch referral page paid in product tiers: 5 friends earned shave cream, 25 a razor set, 50 a year of free blades. 100 thousand emails collected in one week, before a single razor existed.

4. **Jet.com paid its top referrer in stock.** Their pre-launch contest gave the number one referrer 100 thousand shares of the company. One super-motivated insider recruited thousands; everyone else competed harder because the leaderboard was public.

5. **Tesla paid in status and toys, not cash.** Free Supercharging miles, exclusive wheels, invitations to unveilings, and free Roadsters for top referrers. When your customers are fans, prizes beat payouts.

6. **Morning Brew's merch ladder.** 3 referrals earned stickers, then mugs, hoodies, and a trip. Referrals drove roughly a third of their millions of subscribers, and every hoodie was a walking billboard.

7. **Uber and Lyft's give-get ride credits.** Two-sided credits denominated in rides, with unique vanity codes drivers handed out on printed cards. The inviter felt generous, not spammy — that's the psychological unlock.

8. **HelloFresh flooded the world with free boxes.** Every subscriber got free-box codes to hand to friends, and influencers got stacks of them. Meal kits have brutal churn, so they industrialized the warm intro.

9. **Robinhood's free stock lottery.** Both sides of a referral got a random fractional share worth 2 to 200 dollars. The lottery mechanic made a 3 dollar average cost feel like a jackpot ticket.

10. **Monzo's golden tickets.** During the waitlist era, existing users periodically received one golden ticket granting a friend instant access — skipping the queue. Scarcity made a simple invite feel like a gift worth rationing.

## Part 2: Pre-Launch, Waitlists & Invite Scarcity (11–20)

11. **Dropbox's explainer video for Hacker News.** Before the product worked at scale, Drew Houston posted a demo video stuffed with in-jokes for the Digg and Hacker News crowd. The waitlist jumped from 5 thousand to 75 thousand overnight.

12. **Robinhood's ranked waitlist.** A million people pre-launch, each shown their exact position and offered a skip-the-line jump for every referral. The queue itself was the viral product.

13. **Mailbox's live counter.** The email app showed how many people were ahead of you and behind you — 800 thousand deep at peak. Watching your number tick down became a shareable ritual.

14. **Gmail launched invite-only with 1 gigabyte free.** Storage 100 times bigger than Hotmail, gated behind scarce invites that people sold on eBay. Generosity plus scarcity equals status.

15. **Clubhouse rationed invites during lockdown.** Two invites per user, celebrity rooms with Elon Musk, and FOMO screenshots everywhere. It hit a 4 billion dollar valuation on scarcity mechanics — and also shows the ceiling: scarcity without retention deflates.

16. **OnePlus made you earn the right to buy a phone.** Their first flagship was purchasable by invite only. It turned a no-name Chinese manufacturer's supply constraint into a global status game.

17. **Superhuman's concierge waitlist.** A 200 thousand person waitlist, onboarding by personal video call, and the Sean Ellis question — "how disappointed would you be if you couldn't use this?" — deciding who gets in next. Qualification as marketing.

18. **Mint earned trust before launch with content.** A personal-finance blog that out-ranked incumbents, plus "I want Mint" badges bloggers embedded to earn early access. 20 thousand-plus waitlisted users who already believed.

19. **Away sold a book before it had luggage.** When production slipped past the holidays, they sold a travel hardcover bundled with a gift card for the future suitcase. Pre-selling the story kept the launch window alive.

20. **Kickstarter as the launch stage.** Pebble raised 10 million and Oculus 2.4 million in pre-orders — the campaign totals themselves became the press story. Crowdfunding is a waitlist that pays you.

## Part 3: Piggybacking & Platform Arbitrage (21–30)

21. **Airbnb reverse-engineered Craigslist.** A "post to Craigslist" button siphoned the incumbent's audience into richer listings. Riding a bigger network's rails — until they cut you off — is a classic cold-start bridge.

22. **YouTube grew inside MySpace.** The embeddable player let MySpace users decorate profiles with video, and every embed advertised YouTube. The host network supplied the traffic; the parasite kept the accounts.

23. **Zynga farmed early Facebook feeds.** FarmVille requests and notifications exploited the open news feed until Facebook throttled it — but not before Zynga built a 200-million-player empire. Every new platform briefly over-distributes; move fast and plan your exit.

24. **Spotify's 2011 Facebook deal.** Auto-sharing every song you played into friends' feeds, plus Facebook login. Music is identity; broadcasting listening turned private consumption into acquisition.

25. **Instagram shared out to every network it would later beat.** One-tap cross-posting to Twitter, Facebook, Tumblr, and Foursquare meant every beautiful filtered photo elsewhere was an Instagram ad with a link back.

26. **PayPal rode eBay before owning it.** Sellers begged for it, buyers followed, and PayPal even ran a bot that bought auction items and insisted on paying with PayPal. Find the marketplace where your use case already screams.

27. **Google bought the browser defaults.** Billions per year to Apple, plus the old Google Toolbar bundled into other software downloads, plus Chrome promoted on the world's most visited homepage. Defaults are destiny; buy or build the shelf space.

28. **McAfee and Norton preinstalled on every PC.** OEM bundling deals made antivirus a default nag on new machines for a decade. Unsexy, wildly effective.

29. **Netflix put coupons inside DVD-player boxes.** Partnerships with hardware manufacturers seeded free-trial inserts exactly where new DVD owners were unboxing. Attach yourself to the purchase that precedes your use case.

30. **Honey lived at everyone's checkout.** A browser extension that activates at the exact moment of purchase became distribution so valuable PayPal paid 4 billion dollars for it. Ambient utility beats destination sites.

## Part 4: Shareable Artifacts & Data-Driven Virality (31–40)

31. **Spotify Wrapped made data a holiday.** An annual, personal, hyper-shareable recap that floods every social feed each December — plus billboards built from anonymized listening data, like "Dear person who played Sorry 42 times on Valentine's Day."

32. **Wordle's emoji grid.** The genius wasn't the puzzle; it was the spoiler-free green-and-yellow grid you could paste anywhere. Zero-friction, intrigue-generating share artifact — bought by the New York Times within months.

33. **Strava's kudos, segments, and Year in Sport.** Leaderboards for every stretch of road, social applause, and an annual recap card. Exercise becomes content, and content recruits the next athlete.

34. **GitHub's green contribution graph.** A public heatmap of your work that developers embed in resumes and screenshots. A profile that quantifies pride is a profile people evangelize.

35. **Venmo made payments a social feed.** Public transactions with emoji captions turned money transfer into ambient social proof — and made "Venmo me" a verb. The feed was the growth engine, not the transfer.

36. **OkCupid published its dating data.** The OkTrends blog turned anonymized match statistics into viral sociology essays that landed national press and a book deal. Your database is a newsroom.

37. **Zillow's Zestimate gave every home a number.** Instantly controversial, endlessly checkable — everyone looked up their own house and their neighbor's. A provocative data artifact for every address in America is programmatic curiosity.

38. **16Personalities and BuzzFeed quizzes.** Identity-revealing results people can't help posting. BuzzFeed built hundreds of millions of visits on "Which character are you?" — quiz results are self-expression wearing your brand.

39. **Warby Parker's Home Try-On selfies.** Five free frames shipped to your door, and buyers naturally posted photos asking friends to vote. The trial mechanic had a share trigger welded in.

40. **ChatGPT and Midjourney grew on screenshots.** ChatGPT's outputs were inherently screenshot-able wonders; Midjourney ran entirely inside public Discord channels where everyone watched everyone else's generations and learned prompting by osmosis. Make the output the ad and the community the classroom.

## Part 5: Content, SEO & Programmatic Pages (41–50)

41. **Zapier built a landing page for every integration pair.** Hundreds of thousands of programmatic "Connect X with Y" pages capture every long-tail search, co-marketed with the partner apps themselves. Their SEO moat is combinatorial.

42. **Canva owns the word "template."** Dedicated ranking pages for every conceivable design need — resume templates, Instagram post templates, invoice templates — each opening straight into the editor. Search intent to activated user in one click.

43. **Wise's currency-pair pages.** A page for every "dollars to pesos" style conversion query, with a live calculator and transparent fees. Programmatic SEO aimed at moments of transactional intent.

44. **NerdWallet's calculators.** Mortgage, credit card, and savings calculators rank for decades and monetize with affiliate offers. Interactive utility out-ranks prose.

45. **TripAdvisor and Yelp let users write the pages.** Millions of user reviews became millions of indexed landing pages. The network produces the SEO surface; the SEO surface feeds the network.

46. **LinkedIn won the name-search query.** Public profiles rank first for almost any professional's name, so having one became mandatory. Owning ego-search is compounding, free acquisition.

47. **Ahrefs and Moz taught the market they sell to.** Product-led tutorials, Whiteboard Friday videos, and data studies aimed exactly at "how do I do SEO" queries. Teach the job and you become the tool of choice.

48. **Buffer guest-posted its way to its first 100 thousand users.** Around 150 guest posts by the founder in nine months, everywhere startups read. Borrowed audiences, systematically.

49. **Groove and Baremetrics grew on radical transparency.** Public revenue dashboards and a "journey to 100 thousand dollars monthly revenue" blog series. Honest numbers are a content category with almost no competition.

50. **HubSpot bought a newsletter instead of renting ads.** Acquiring The Hustle bought them a million-plus daily readers and a media arm. When channel prices spike, owning the audience beats bidding for it.

## Part 6: Engineering-as-Marketing: Free Tools (51–60)

51. **HubSpot's Website Grader.** A free site-audit tool that generated millions of leads by scoring the thing marketers care most about — their own site — and mapping every weakness to a HubSpot product.

52. **CoSchedule's Headline Analyzer.** One free micro-tool became the top result for headline help and an evergreen lead magnet. A single-purpose tool can out-market a whole blog.

53. **Shopify's name and logo generators.** Free business-name and logo makers catch founders at the exact moment before they need a store. Serve the step immediately upstream of your product.

54. **Adobe gave the Reader away to sell the writer.** Free Acrobat Reader made PDF the universal document standard, and then creation tools printed money. Subsidize consumption, charge for production.

55. **Square handed out free card readers.** The hardware was the customer-acquisition cost; the take rate on payments repaid it forever. Give away the razor when you own the blades.

56. **Stripe made documentation the storefront.** Legendarily clean docs, a seven-line integration demo, and Stripe Atlas incorporating startups from anywhere — acquiring companies at their birth certificate.

57. **Credit Karma gave away the credit score.** Free forever, refreshed constantly, against incumbents charging monthly. The free score built a data flywheel that matched users to loan offers.

58. **Unsplash gave photographers distribution and everyone free photos.** Every free image credit spread the brand across the internet's blogs and decks. Give-away inventory that travels.

59. **Glassdoor's give-to-get wall.** You see salaries only after you post yours. The gate manufactures the exact content the next visitor comes for — Levels.fyi and Blind ran the same trade, with Blind adding work-email verification for spicy anonymity.

60. **Duolingo made the free tier genuinely complete.** No paywall on learning itself — monetization came later from subscriptions removing friction. Half a billion downloads flowed from being the un-gated option in a paywalled category.

## Part 7: Freemium, Pricing & Packaging Plays (61–70)

61. **Zoom's 40-minute ceiling.** Free calls capped at 40 minutes, one minute shorter than most meetings run. The upgrade prompt arrives mid-conversation, in front of witnesses, at maximum inconvenience.

62. **Slack's fair billing.** Automatic credits for inactive seats — the anti-enterprise-software move. Trust became the pricing page's best feature and IT's favorite talking point.

63. **Amazon Prime as a profitable loss.** Pay upfront for free shipping and you psychologically must route every purchase through Amazon to amortize it. Prepaid membership converts customers into monopolists on your behalf.

64. **Costco's 1 dollar 50 hot dog and free samples.** Loss-leader theater that anchors the entire value perception of the membership. Sampling reliably lifts sales; the hot dog is a marketing budget you eat.

65. **Atlassian priced low enough to skip salespeople.** Transparent self-serve pricing, no cold calls, and the savings poured into R&D and a marketplace. The absence of a sales team was the differentiator enterprise buyers whispered about.

66. **Chase Sapphire Reserve made a credit card drop.** A 100 thousand point signup bonus plus a heavy metal card that ran out of stock — a waitlist for a credit card. Scarcity and status engineered onto a commodity.

67. **Amex Centurion built mystique on an invite you can't request.** The black card acquired customers for every other Amex tier by existing as a rumor. Your top tier's job is storytelling, not revenue.

68. **Chime led with payday two days early.** One structural feature — early direct deposit — became the entire acquisition pitch for millions of users. Find the pricing-adjacent feature that headlines itself.

69. **AppSumo lifetime deals as launch capital.** Early SaaS companies sold discounted lifetime plans to a deal-hungry audience for cash flow, reviews, and thousands of vocal early users. Sell the future once to fund the present.

70. **Groupon's deals only "tipped" at critical mass.** Early deals activated only if enough people bought, so buyers recruited friends to unlock their own discount. Collective-action pricing turns customers into your street team.

## Part 8: Gamification, Streaks & Retention Mechanics (71–80)

71. **Duolingo's streak industrial complex.** Streaks, streak freezes, weekend amulets, leagues, and a home-screen widget with an increasingly distressed owl. Loss aversion, engineered in layers, drives daily active behavior at nine-figure scale.

72. **Snapchat streaks made retention a two-player pact.** Breaking a streak lets a friend down, not just the app. Social obligation is stickier than any notification.

73. **Foursquare's mayorships and badges.** Location check-ins became a turf war for local status. Arbitrary titles, fiercely defended — status is the cheapest reward you can mint.

74. **McDonald's Monopoly.** A sweepstakes stapled to fries that measurably lifts visit frequency every run. Collection mechanics create artificial almost-winning — you're always one Boardwalk short.

75. **Starbucks' stars and gold tier.** The rewards app holds billions in preloaded balances — customers literally bank with Starbucks for status and free drinks. Points plus tiers plus mobile ordering equals habit infrastructure.

76. **Peloton's leaderboard and high-fives.** Live class rankings and mid-ride encouragement bolt competitive social pressure onto a stationary bike. The hardware is exercise; the leaderboard is the retention product.

77. **Wordle's one-per-day scarcity.** A single daily puzzle synchronized the whole world's play and made the game impossible to binge and burn out on. Rationing content created ritual.

78. **Robinhood's confetti.** Celebration animations on trades made investing feel like a game — enormously effective, then criticized and rolled back. Gamifying consequential decisions works, and that's exactly why regulators noticed.

79. **Domino's Pizza Tracker.** Turning order status into a five-stage progress bar made waiting for pizza engaging and reorder behavior habitual. Instrument the wait, not just the purchase.

80. **Epic gave away a game every week.** Free weekly titles trained tens of millions to open the Epic Games Store on schedule. A recurring gift is a recurring habit hook — inventory as retention spend.

## Part 9: Community, Ambassadors & Superusers (81–90)

81. **Reddit's entire marketing budget was 500 dollars of stickers.** Alexis Ohanian slapped them on poles and laptops city by city. Communities recruit themselves once seeded; merch made early users feel like members of a movement.

82. **Harley-Davidson chartered the Harley Owners Group.** A company-run riders club with local chapters, rallies, and patches — a million-plus members who make leaving the brand feel like leaving family.

83. **Sephora's Beauty Insider community.** Points, tiers, and a forum where members answer each other's product questions all day. The community does pre-sales support while accumulating switching costs.

84. **Lululemon's ambassador network.** Local yoga instructors got free gear and studio spotlighting; their classes became distribution. Recruit the people your customers already follow, one neighborhood at a time.

85. **Peloton's member Facebook group.** Hundreds of thousands of members generating accountability, tribes, and hashtag rides — user-run retention the company only had to bless.

86. **Gymshark seeded lifters before "influencer" was a word.** Free apparel to YouTube fitness creators, then expo meetups where online fans queued for hours. They grew a billion-dollar brand off relationships incumbents ignored.

87. **Glossier built the audience before the product.** The Into The Gloss blog interviewed thousands of women about routines for four years; readers co-designed the first products and became its rep network. Audience first, catalog second.

88. **Xiaomi's Mi Fans co-developed the OS.** Weekly software updates shaped by forum feedback, flash sales as fan events, and festivals for superusers. Community as R&D plus community as launch demand.

89. **Salesforce turned customers into "Trailblazers."** Free Trailhead certifications, ranks, and badges people put on LinkedIn — a career identity anchored to one vendor's ecosystem. Certify people and they defend the platform in every vendor evaluation.

90. **Nextdoor mailed actual postcards.** Neighborhood launches required around ten founding households, and paper invites carried a real neighbor's name for trust. Offline social proof to bootstrap an online neighborhood.

## Part 10: Influencer, Creator & Social Seeding (91–100)

91. **Daniel Wellington built a watch empire on micro-influencers.** Thousands of free watches to small Instagram accounts, each with a personal discount code. Volume plus tracking codes beat celebrity one-offs on price and proof.

92. **Fashion Nova posted like a media company.** Thousands of influencer collaborations and dozens of posts daily, with manufacturing fast enough to chase whatever spiked. Feed velocity as strategy.

93. **Stanley let the fans lead.** The Quencher was nearly discontinued until a women's shopping blog proved the demographic; Stanley leaned in, and when a woman's car burned leaving the cup intact, the CEO gifted her a new car on TikTok. Revenue went from about 70 million to 750 million.

94. **Duolingo's unhinged owl.** The brand mascot became a chaotic TikTok character with over 10 million followers, riding trends within hours. A personality, not a content calendar.

95. **Wendy's roasted its way to relevance.** Savage Twitter replies and National Roast Day made a burger chain must-follow entertainment — and one teen's #NuggsForCarter plea became the most retweeted tweet on earth.

96. **e.l.f. Cosmetics commissioned an original song.** "Eyes Lips Face" became one of TikTok's most viral branded challenges ever, with billions of views from users dancing to an ad they didn't experience as an ad.

97. **Chipotle's #GuacDance challenge.** A TikTok dance for National Avocado Day pulled 250 thousand video submissions and drove their biggest guacamole day ever. Give the internet homework it wants to do.

98. **Ocean Spray moved at meme speed.** When Nathan Apodaca skateboarded to Fleetwood Mac sipping cran-raspberry, the company gifted him a truck within days instead of sending lawyers. Riding organic moments beats manufacturing them.

99. **Cash App made Fridays a giveaway ritual.** #CashAppFriday cash drops on Twitter trained millions to engage weekly, while rappers name-dropped cashtags in lyrics. Direct cash as content, plus culture as distribution.

100. **MrBeast made generosity the format.** Videos structured around giving away houses and islands turned marketing spend into the entertainment itself, then funneled attention into Feastables chocolate at retail. The ad budget is the show.

## Part 11: Stunts, Guerrilla & PR Moments (101–110)

101. **Salesforce hired fake protesters at Siebel's conference.** Actors waved "No Software" signs outside the incumbent's user event while Benioff's team handed press releases to arriving journalists. Positioning theater, executed on someone else's stage.

102. **Burger King's Whopper Detour.** The app sold a one-cent Whopper — but only if you ordered while standing inside a McDonald's geofence. 1.5 million downloads and a Cannes award for weaponized location data.

103. **Red Bull jumped from space.** The Stratos stratosphere dive drew 8 million concurrent YouTube viewers and defined the brand's "gives you wings" claim with an actual event. Own a moment, not a slot.

104. **ALS Ice Bucket Challenge's nomination chain.** Dump, donate, and publicly nominate three friends within 24 hours. The deadline plus name-calling mechanic raised 115 million dollars in a summer — the most successful viral loop ever run for charity.

105. **Cards Against Humanity sold literal nothing.** One Black Friday they removed the store and sold "nothing" for 5 dollars — 30 thousand people paid. Another year they sold boxes of actual bull feces. Anti-marketing as performance, covered by every outlet.

106. **IHOP became IHOb.** A fake name change to the International House of Burgers generated tens of thousands of media stories for a burger line launch. Manufactured outrage, fully reversible.

107. **Oreo dunked in the dark.** When the Super Bowl blacked out, Oreo tweeted "You can still dunk in the dark" within minutes and won the night over every 5 million dollar ad. Newsjacking is a speed sport — pre-authorize the team.

108. **Aviation Gin answered the Peloton ad in 72 hours.** They hired the actress from the controversial Peloton commercial for a sequel where friends toast her freedom. Reactive casting turned someone else's PR crisis into their brand moment.

109. **Wise marched underwear-clad through the City of London.** "Nothing to hide" protests against hidden bank fees — cheap, photogenic, and perfectly on-message for transparent pricing.

110. **Taco Bell blacked itself out.** They deleted every social account for a day, replaced with "the new way to Taco Bell isn't on Twitter, it's #onlyintheapp." Self-imposed silence drove app installs better than shouting.

## Part 12: Brand, Packaging & Product-as-Marketing (111–120)

111. **Liquid Death made water a metal band.** Tallboy cans, "murder your thirst," and an album made from its own hate comments. Selling identity and jokes made a 700 million dollar valuation out of a commodity — water.

112. **Coca-Cola printed your name on the bottle.** Share a Coke swapped the logo for first names, and people hunted shelves for themselves and friends, posting finds. Mass personalization is a UGC machine.

113. **RXBAR put the ingredient list on the front.** "3 egg whites, 6 almonds, no B.S." as the entire package design. Radical clarity was the differentiation — and the ad.

114. **Apple made unboxing a genre.** Packaging engineered so precisely that opening it became millions of YouTube videos, plus "Shot on iPhone" billboards made from customers' photos. The customer's first 30 seconds and best photo are both campaigns.

115. **Glossier's pink bubble pouch.** Reusable, photogenic packaging that appeared organically in thousands of Instagram shelfies. Design the container to be the content.

116. **In-N-Out's secret menu.** Animal Style isn't on any board — ordering it performs insider status, and initiating newcomers is word-of-mouth with a script. Secrets recruit.

117. **Patagonia told people not to buy its jacket.** The famous full-page ad, and REI closing on Black Friday for #OptOutside, converted anti-consumerist values into loyalty and — yes — more sales.

118. **Domino's confessed its pizza was bad.** The Pizza Turnaround campaign aired real criticism, rebuilt the recipe publicly, and the stock became one of the decade's best performers. Public honesty about flaws buys attention nothing else can.

119. **Chewy sends flowers and paintings.** Handwritten cards, condolence flowers when a pet dies, surprise pet portraits — each story detonates on social media. Extravagant service moments are cheaper than the reach they earn.

120. **Mailchimp turned a mispronunciation into a campaign.** After the Serial podcast's famous "MailKimp" ad, they made nine absurdist films — JailBlimp, KaleLimp — leaning into the joke. Own your meme before the internet does.

## Part 13: Partnerships, Bundling & Distribution Deals (121–130)

121. **Microsoft bundled Teams into Office.** Included free with the suite companies already paid for, Teams reversed Slack's momentum inside two years. Distribution attached to an existing contract is nearly unbeatable — Slack's antitrust complaint said as much.

122. **Spotify bundled with Hulu and student plans.** Discounted student bundles captured users at habit-formation age and made unsubscribing mean losing two services. Bundles raise the exit toll.

123. **T-Mobile paid your breakup fee.** The Un-carrier campaign bought out competitors' early-termination fees, removing the single biggest switching barrier while the CEO trash-talked rivals on stage. Pay the switching cost yourself.

124. **Mint Mobile put its owner in the ads.** Ryan Reynolds bought a stake and wrote self-aware cheap-looking commercials fronted by himself. Celebrity as equity partner outperforms celebrity as hired face — the incentive shows.

125. **Fortnite hosted Travis Scott.** A 12-million-concurrent in-game concert plus endless crossover skins turned a game into a venue other brands pay to enter. Become the platform where culture happens.

126. **Supreme and Nike weaponized the collab.** Limited crossovers — KFC Crocs, Oreo Supreme — generate headlines and resale frenzies far beyond the units sold. A collab borrows two audiences and manufactures scarcity news.

127. **Telegram and Signal surfed WhatsApp's stumble.** When WhatsApp announced a privacy-policy change, rivals converted the backlash — helped by a two-word Musk tweet, "Use Signal" — into tens of millions of installs in days. Position yourself as the ready alternative before the incident.

128. **Paytm blitzed India's demonetization.** When large banknotes were voided overnight, Paytm covered newspapers and shop stalls with QR codes within days. When policy shocks demand, be violently available.

129. **Jio gave a nation free data.** Six months of free unlimited 4G brought 100 million subscribers in 170 days, the fastest telecom ramp ever. Free at nation scale, priced by the balance sheet of an industrial giant.

130. **DocuSign let the product recruit the counterparty.** Every contract sent forces a recipient to experience the product at a moment of professional necessity. B2B virality hides inside workflows that cross company walls.

## Part 14: Marketplace, Local & Emerging-Market Mechanics (131–140)

131. **Airbnb sent photographers to hosts' homes.** Free professional photography doubled some listings' bookings and raised the whole marketplace's perceived quality. Subsidize supply-side polish; it converts demand.

132. **Pinduoduo made shopping a team sport.** Team-purchase pricing unlocked only when you recruited friends via WeChat groups, plus "help me cut the price" links and a farm game shipping real fruit. Zero to 300 million users in about three years on social commerce mechanics.

133. **Temu gamified the invite grind.** Spin-the-wheel credits, "just one more friend" cash thresholds, and casino loops — Pinduoduo's playbook exported globally and backed by billions in ads. Effective, and a masterclass in how far incentive mechanics can push before they feel predatory.

134. **WeChat's red envelope sneak attack.** Digitizing Lunar New Year cash gifts — then a shake-your-phone giveaway on the CCTV gala watched by 700 million — let WeChat Pay leapfrog Alipay's eight-year head start in one holiday. Ride a ritual.

135. **CRED gamified paying your credit card bill.** Rewards, lotteries, and IPL cricket ads for the one financial chore everyone does monthly. Wrap status and play around an existing obligation.

136. **Zerodha spent zero on advertising, ever.** India's largest stock broker grew on referrals, a free education portal called Varsity, and product reliability. In trust businesses, the absence of hype can be the brand.

137. **Booking.com industrialized urgency.** "Two rooms left," "booked 4 times today," countdown timers — relentless scarcity cues that lifted conversion so well regulators eventually forced softening. Urgency works; calibrate before the watchdogs do.

138. **Groupon's daily deal made local a broadcast event.** One deal per city per day, delivered by email at breakfast, created appointment commerce and the fastest-growing revenue ramp of its era — and its collapse warns what happens when the deal is the only retention.

139. **Uber's playbook press stunts, localized.** On-demand ice cream trucks, kittens for cuddling, boats and helicopters — each city launch got a media-bait service variant that taught the on-demand behavior. PR stunts doubling as product tutorials.

140. **Nike SNKRS turned buying shoes into a raffle.** Draws for hyped drops made losing common, winning shareable, and checking the app habitual. The purchase became a game you mostly lose — and keep playing.

## Part 15: B2B & Product-Led Growth Plays (141–150)

141. **Slack's wall of love.** The company retweeted customer praise from a dedicated account and obsessed over delight details; word-of-mouth carried it to the fastest SaaS ramp of its era with no sales team for years.

142. **Calendly and Loom made every use an exposure.** Booking links and video links land in outsiders' inboxes as functional necessities — each one a live demo with the sender's credibility attached. Pick features that only work by leaving the building.

143. **Atlassian and GitLab let open source open doors.** Free and open tiers seeded developers who dragged the paid product into their employers. Bottom-up adoption inverts the sales funnel: the champion is already inside.

144. **Notion cultivated a template economy.** Creator-made templates, an affiliate program, and community translations meant thousands of people sold Notion for their own reasons. Make your users' side hustle your distribution.

145. **Figma shipped multiplayer and Config.** Browser-based zero-install collaboration meant inviting a colleague was the workflow, and its community files plus the Config conference made designers the marketing department. The file itself was viral.

146. **HubSpot certified a generation of marketers.** Free Academy courses and certifications became résumé currency displayed on LinkedIn — every badge an endorsement, every certified marketer a future buyer who already knows the tool.

147. **Gong published its sales-call data.** Insights mined from millions of recorded calls — "talk ratios of top reps," "words that kill deals" — became irresistible LinkedIn content for exactly their buyers. Your product's exhaust is your content engine.

148. **Basecamp and Intercom wrote the books.** Rework, Getting Real, and Intercom's product guides positioned founders as the philosophers of their category. A book is a business card that argues for you at scale.

149. **Twilio put "Ask Your Developer" on billboards.** Developer evangelists, hackathons, and ads addressed to executives via their engineers. When developers choose the tools, market to the chooser and arm them to convince the payer.

150. **Salesforce's Dreamforce swallowed San Francisco.** A user conference grown into a 170 thousand-attendee city takeover that manufactures a week of industry news annually. At sufficient scale, your event becomes your category's calendar — and everyone else plans around it.

---

*Patterns worth noticing across all 150: the reward denominated in product rather than cash; the share trigger welded into core use; scarcity converting attention into status; give-to-get gates manufacturing content; free tools upstream of purchase intent; and speed — most famous "moments" were responses executed in under 72 hours.*
